Abstract
The discourse on climate change transcends scientific discussions and policy debates, of¬≠
ten incorporating moral language and ethical considerations. This study explores framing 
strategies in political persuasion and the underlying moral foundations associated with 
climate change by combining computational methods and critical discourse analysis of 
tweets from 111th-117th U.S. Congresses members.¬†The aim is to map out the bipartisan 
trends in the use of moral language and framing concerning climate change issues, thereby 
enriching the understanding of public opinion dynamics and the evolving partisan divide 
on climate action. Our findings reveal an intensifying partisan polarization in framing. 
Contrary to the expected moral divide, we uncover a bipartisan agreement on the moral 
foundation of care and fairness, and a consistent cross-party employment of moral lan¬≠
guage associated with frames over time. The interplay between generic frames and moral 
foundations suggests the potential for collective action on climate change across the po¬≠
litical spectrum.

The discourse surrounding climate change is not only a matter of scientific consensus and 
policy debate but is also deeply intertwined with moral and ethical considerations (Mar¬≠
kowitz and Shariff 2012). As the global community grapples with the escalating impacts 
of climate change, the moral dimension of environmental stewardship has become increas¬≠
ingly prominent. This intersection of morality and climate discourse has garnered signifi¬≠
cant academic interest, leading researchers to explore how moral concerns are articulated in 
the context of climate change and how these articulations influence public perception and 
policy preferences.
Moral language cues have been identified as an important factor in guiding individuals‚Äô 
attention (Brady et al. 2019). Moral language, and the ways that language is used to frame 
issues along moral lines, can activate and intensify beliefs, and change the types of deci¬≠
sions and policies people are willing to accept (Anderson et al. 2013). Furthermore, as moral 
values are associated with social and political identity and represent politicized discourse 
(Wetts 2020), the use of moral frames can trigger in-group identification and loyalty, thereby 
yielding stronger persuasive effects (Hurst and Stern 2020). A pivotal area of study within 
this realm focuses on the moral frameworks employed by political elites to reinforce soli¬≠
darity within their respective political factions. Existing research (e.g., Graham et al. 2009; 
Sterling and Jost 2018) highlights how moral language as a vehicle for conveying moral 
values plays a crucial role in the persuasive tactics of political figures aiming to galvanize 
their base.
However, the moral divide between conservatives and liberals is by no means absolute 
(Dickinson et al. 2016) or binary (Graham et al. 2009). Sterling and Jost (2018) found in an 
analysis of congressional tweets from 2014 that Democrats, in addition to using moral val¬≠
ues of care and fairness (‚Äúindividualizing foundations‚ÄĚ), also employed purity and loyalty-
related language (‚Äúbinding foundations‚ÄĚ), which is assumed to be more commonly used by 
Republicans. Day et al. (2014) found that when general pro-environmental appeals matched 
the moral values of conservatives, they had consistent positive effects across an array of 
environmental attitudes and behaviors (see also Wolsko et al. 2016). More broadly, recent 
research found no evidence for a consistent pattern of ideological differences in the use of 
moral language across topics (Wang and Inbar 2021).
The debate over climate change constitutes an illuminating case for the complex strate¬≠
gies politicians employ to shape public opinion and policy. Central to this dialogue are the 
notions of generic frames and moral language, which serve as critical tools in the arse¬≠
nal of political communication. Generic frames offer a lens through which complex issues 
are simplified and made relatable to the public, facilitating engagement with and under¬≠
standing of the climate crisis. Moral language, on the other hand, appeals to the ethical 
values and principles of individuals, evoking emotional responses and moral judgments 
that can significantly sway public opinion and drive policy initiatives. For example, Vainio 
and M√§kiniemi (2016) found that there were differences between general and issue-specific 
moral concerns, which have implications for their association with political orientation 
and pro-environmental behavior. The endorsement of general moral foundations made the 
effect of political orientation on climate-friendly choices¬†disappear. They further developed 
an issue-specific measure of moral foundations and found that binding foundations (i.e., 
ingroup/loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanctity) were associated with the avoidance 
of climate-friendly choices among right-wing individuals.

This study explores the moral foundations and framing strategies used in political per¬≠
suasion regarding climate change in the U.S., where the issue remains highly politicized. 
It underscores the divergent approaches stemming from broader ideological belief systems 
and their alignment with political parties in the U.S. Specifically, the study highlights that 
political liberals, who tend to align with the Democratic Party, generally acknowledge and 
advocate for policies addressing anthropogenic climate change, whereas political con¬≠
servatives, who are more often associated with the Republican Party, have demonstrated 
significant skepticism and resistance to such policies over the last two decades (Andrews 
and Smirnov 2020; McCright and Dunlap 2011). While this correlation is not absolute‚ÄĒ
acknowledging that not all Democrats are liberal and not all Republicans are conserva¬≠
tive‚ÄĒit reflects a prevailing trend in U.S. politics.1 This division is sometimes attributed 
to different moral foundations that underpin the political spectrum (e.g., Park et al. 2010). 
Drawing on Moral Foundations Theory (MFT), previous studies has shown that the political 
left tends to emphasize values of fairness and care (‚Äúindividualizing foundations‚ÄĚ), whereas 
the right prioritizes loyalty, sanctity, and authority (‚Äúbinding foundations‚ÄĚ) (e.g., Kugler 
et al. 2014). This dichotomy in moral prioritization not only shapes political identities but 
also informs the strategies employed in political messaging. Such targeted communication, 
adopting the specific moral languages resonant with these foundational values, has been 
demonstrated to effectively engage and motivate policy compliance across the ideological 
spectrum (cf. Ryan 2014; Salomon et al. 2017; Skitka and Bauman 2008).
The objective of this study is to map out the bipartisan trends in moral language and 
framing of climate change issues, thereby enriching the understanding of public opinion 
dynamics and the evolving partisan divide on climate action. The distinction in moral lan¬≠
guage and framing between liberals and conservatives points to a deeper ideological divide 
in perceptions of environmental responsibility and ethical obligation. It underscores the role 
of moral foundations in shaping political discourse on climate change, suggesting that effec¬≠
tive communication strategies may need to account for these underlying moral differences.
Moreover, our analysis aims to uncover common concerns and moral foundations regard¬≠
ing climate change that transcend party lines. By understanding the moral underpinnings 
of climate discourse, policymakers, activists, and communicators can craft more resonant 
messages that bridge ideological divides and foster a more inclusive approach to address¬≠
ing the global climate crisis. There is a heightened need for research to explore the nuanced 
relationship between moral language, socioeconomic themes, and climate change discourse. 
Such investigations could provide invaluable insights into how moral foundations influence 
framing strategies and, in turn, public engagement with climate issues.

1‚Äā Theoretical background
1.1‚Äā Moral foundations, political persuasion and environmental issues
The discourse on climate change often intersects with both moral and material consider¬≠
ations, underscoring the complexity of persuasion strategies employed by political actors 
(Wolsko et al. 2016). Two independent yet interrelated dimensions of moral foundation 
expressions are worth noting: moral values and moral language use. Moral values justify in-
group beliefs with sacred principles, resulting in different usage of moral language to defend 
the group identity and outcast ‚Äúwicked‚ÄĚ outgroup members (Wang and Inbar 2021). Moral 
language is a tool often used by politicians to influence public debates and persuade voters 
(Clifford and Jerit 2013). Since voters tend to associate an issue with their moral values, 
morality can then function as a motivator for actions (Fiske and Rai 2014).
On the issue of climate change specifically, moralization is arguably a cause for the wide¬≠
spread political polarization (Feinberg and Willer 2013). Liberals are more likely to view 
climate change as a moral issue than conservatives, and are more likely to consider a range 
of specific pro-environmental behaviors (e.g., recycling, buying green products, and using 
reusable bags) to be ‚Äúmoral‚ÄĚ (Currie and Choma 2018). To closely examine the moral com¬≠
munication surrounding climate change, we first review existing theories of moral values, 
and then explore the scholarship on moral language use.
One of the most influential theories concerning moral values is the Moral Foundations 
Theory (MFT), a comprehensive framework that delineates fundamental moral values uni¬≠
versally recognized across cultures. The MFT identifies five core moral domains: harm/care, 
fairness/reciprocity, ingroup/loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanctity. Of these, harm/
care and fairness/reciprocity are categorized as individualizing values due to their emphasis 
on the rights of individuals. In contrast, ingroup/loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanc¬≠
tity are labeled as binding values, highlighting cohesion within groups through duties and 
loyalty (Van Leeuwen and Park 2009). The expressions of these moral values play a pivotal 
role in policy persuasion, as they align closely with the political ideologies that influence 
individuals‚Äô behaviors and attitudes towards policies and governmental issues. This correla¬≠
tion has been explored extensively in political communication studies, revealing that liber¬≠
als generally prioritize individualizing foundations, while conservatives are more inclined 
towards binding foundations (Miles 2016).

A similar distinction is also manifested in the discussion over climate change. Much of 
the political discourse on climate change aligns with individualizing foundations, such as 
harm and fairness, which may resonate more strongly with political liberals. Democrats 
typically employ themes of intergenerational equity, sustainability, and moral obligations to 
nature, leveraging the harm and fairness foundations to advocate for environmental actions 
(Nisbet 2009; Zhou 2016). Conversely, Republicans are less likely to view pro-environ¬≠
mental actions through a moral lens (Markowitz and Shariff 2012). When they need to 
emphasize the moral implications of climate change, Republicans may resort to binding 
foundations like in-group loyalty and deference to authority, reflecting a distinct moral per¬≠
spective (Markowitz 2012; Markowitz and Shariff 2012).
In addition to the distinction between parties, bipartisan moral values in environmental 
discussions present a unique case where traditional ideological distinctions may occasion¬≠
ally become blurred. Feinberg and Willer‚Äôs (2013) study illustrates that when environmental 
policies are framed in terms of purity/sanctity, the typical ideological differences diminish. 
This suggests that environmental degradation, viewed as a desecration of the world, can 
evoke a strong moral response of disgust that transcends political affiliations. Addition¬≠
ally, Wolsko and colleagues (2016) demonstrated that conservatives could be persuaded 
by pro-environmental appeals in line with their moral values, indicating that the moral 
divide between political parties may be more flexible than previously believed. Therefore, 
a thorough examination of the moral values evoked by political elites regarding climate 
change may provide important insights. Theoretically, this exploration could enhance our 
understanding of the established relationship between political ideologies and moral values. 
Practically, it offers guidance on how to strategically articulate different moral concerns to 
garner broader support. Therefore, we propose the following research question:
RQ1: What moral values do Republican and Democratic members of Congress express in 
their communications about climate change?
H1: Social media posts (tweets) from Democrats will contain a higher percentage of terms 
related to individualizing values, whereas tweets from Republicans will feature a greater 
percentage of terms associated with binding values.
The divergence in moral values between the two parties even extends to their moral lan¬≠
guage use in discussions of vice (moral violations) versus virtue (morally righteous actions). 
Studies have identified a pattern where liberals are more inclined to employ terms associ¬≠
ated with virtue, whereas conservatives tend to favor terms related to vice. This tendency 
was highlighted in a comprehensive analysis of U.S. congressional records from 1994 to 
2016, revealing that Democrats more frequently used positive (virtue) words, especially 
in the context of ingroup loyalty, while Republicans were more prone to using negative 
(vice) words (Takikawa and Sakamoto 2020). Regarding climate change, specifically, cli¬≠
mate skeptics were found to prefer vice terms such as ‚Äúbad‚ÄĚ and ‚Äúfalse,‚ÄĚ while still using 
virtue terms like ‚Äútruth,‚ÄĚ ‚Äúintegrity,‚ÄĚ and ‚Äútrust‚ÄĚ when questioning the validity of scientific 
research on climate change. In comparison, climate change believers strongly endorsed vir¬≠
tue terms (Diakopoulos et al. 2014). Another study (Fulgoni et al. 2016) found that liberals 
were more likely to use harm-vice, fairness-virtue and loyalty-vice language when discuss¬≠
ing climate change, as compared to conservatives, who used authority-vice more intensively

1.2‚Äā The role of generic frames in congress members‚Äô tweets on the climate change
In addition to the moral foundations, generic frames of issues also play a distinct role in 
shaping public opinion and policy outcomes. Generic frames refer to emphasis frames that 
make certain aspects of an issue more salient than others, particularly those related to practi¬≠
cality, cost, and self-interest (Slothuus and De Vreese 2010). These frames are instrumental 
in influencing how political issues are perceived, attributing causes and responsibilities, 
shaping the perceived importance of issues, and ultimately affecting policy decisions (Cac¬≠
ciatore et al. 2016; McLeod et al. 2022). Their utility lies in their ability to connect complex 
issues, such as climate change, with everyday concerns and considerations, thereby making 
them more accessible and relevant to the general public.
Moral foundations of public discourse vary, depending on issues and contexts (e.g., see 
Graham et al. 2009; Wang and Inbar 2021; Frimer et al. 2017). In the realm of climate 
change discourse, the ways political parties frame the issue has profound implications for 
public opinion and policy preferences. Democratic and Republican administrations priori¬≠
tize different concerns and aspects of the overall climate change issue (Klyza and Sousa 
2013). Specifically, Democrats tend to feature testimony from more pro-environment activ¬≠
ists and mainstream scientists, whereas Republicans primarily report testimony from speak¬≠
ers in the business and industrial sectors who argue against climate change regulation (Park 
et al. 2010).
In his 2009 study, Nisbet identified prevalent frameworks used by Democrats and 
Republicans to discuss climate change, highlighting a strategic division in their narrative 
approaches. Democratic leaders often employ frames like ‚ÄúRunaway Science,‚ÄĚ which under¬≠
score the imminent threat of climate change, and ‚ÄúPublic Health,‚ÄĚ spotlighting the negative 
impacts of climate change on environmental quality and public health. These narratives 
convey a unified message: the climate crisis affects us all, necessitating collective action 
against its dire consequences, such as increased infectious diseases, respiratory issues, and 
extreme weather events.
Conversely, Republican leaders favor frames like ‚ÄúScientific Uncertainty,‚ÄĚ suggesting 
climate change is an exaggeration or a manifestation of liberal alarmism, and ‚ÄúMiddle Way,‚ÄĚ 
advocating for bipartisan consensus and balancing economic and environmental interests 
through clean energy and national security enhancements. Additionally, the ‚ÄúEconomic 
Consequences‚ÄĚ frame emphasizes that climate policies would result in unfair burden on 
Americans in international climate agreements if other countries do not take similar actions, 
and the ‚ÄúPublic Accountability‚ÄĚ frame highlights that the government is prioritizing politics 
over climate change science. Nisbet‚Äôs compilation of these framing strategies, drawn from

Previous research has found that politicians from the minority party tend to use more moral 
language than those from the majority in the Congress (Wang and Inbar 2021). Specifically, 
Wang and Inbar (2021) observed that congressional Democrats used more moral language 
than congressional Republicans across all moral foundations, including binding founda¬≠
tions, between 2016 and 2018. Notably, the differences increased after the election of Don¬≠
ald Trump. Debates exist regarding whether incumbency and party positioning in Congress 
significantly affect the use of virtue and vice languages. Takikawa and Sakamoto (2020) 
argue that majority or minority status alone does not result in substantial differences in 
the use of moral languages. Moreover, other studies have shown that the broader political 
climate such as elections can amplify the use of moral language. For example, Sterling and 
Jost (2018) demonstrated that in times of policy shifts, both majority and minority parties 
might resort to moral language to justify their positions and criticize their opponents.
Environmental issues including climate change were traditionally seen as one of major 
concerns of the Democratic Party. Democratic congress members‚Äô engagement in climate 
change issues remains stable, while the participation of Republicans tended to fluctuate 
over time, depending on presidential autonomy (DeGregorio 1998). Fisher and colleagues 
(2013a) showed that during the 110th Congress when Democrats were in the majority, only 
5% Republicans had climate change statements (p. 80). However, this number rose to 24% 
in the 109th Congress when Republicans controlled both the House and Senate. Mean¬≠
while, between the two congresses, the percentages of climate change statements provided 
by Democrats changed only slightly.
Although participation of Republican members of Congress in climate change discourse 
has traditionally lagged, this may be changing. Though they still pursue a different climate 
agenda than Democrats, they appear more willing to consider climate policies than before.

2‚Äā Materials and methods
This study examines hypotheses and research questions by analyzing archived tweets from 
members of the U.S. Congress. Our methodological approach is grounded in Critical Dis¬≠
course Analysis (CDA), which suggests that the linguistic behavior is shaped by social 
affiliations and relationships (Fairclough 1995). Within the CDA framework, discourse is 
conceptualized as a social construct (Wodak and Meyer 2015), and texts are analyzed within 
their cultural and political contexts (Olausson 2009). This perspective allows linguistic anal¬≠
ysis to serve as a powerful tool for examining the ideological processes that influence power 
dynamics and control mechanisms (Fowler and Kress 1979). In this study, we employed 
automated computational techniques for linguistic analysis to investigate both thematic 
(macro-level) and stylistic (micro-level) aspects of discourse (Olausson 2009). To analyze 
the ideological beliefs of Congress members and their moral foundations expressions and 
framing strategies in the political persuasion regarding climate change, we focused on their 
party identification, specifically their affiliation with political parties such as the Democrats 
or Republicans.

Tweets were collected using the official Twitter Historical PowerTrack API. The inclusion 
criteria were as follows: (1) Tweets must contain at least one of five predetermined climate-
related keywords (climate, warming, green, energy, pollution); (2) Tweets must have been 
posted between January 1, 2009, and January 1, 2023, covering the tenure of the 111th 
to 117th Congresses; (3) Tweets must originate from verified accounts; (4) Only original 
tweets were considered, excluding replies and retweets; (5) Tweets must be in English. The 
raw data underwent preprocessing to retain only essential information, such as the creation

2.2‚Äā Moral foundations assessment
Guided by Moral Foundations Theory (MFT), we applied the extended Moral Foundations 
Dictionary (eMFD) (Hopp et al. 2021) to assign virtue and vice probability scores to each 
tweet based on the five moral foundations (i.e., harm/care, fairness/reciprocity, ingroup/
loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanctity). The eMFD computes the morality values 
contained in a given text by referencing pre-established dictionaries. The creation of moral 
foundations dictionaries for textual content analysis has been proposed in previous studies 
(Graham and Haidt 2012; Frimer et al. 2017). Unlike previous manual selection methods for 
dictionary construction, the eMFD employs crowd-sourced annotations to ensure the lexi¬≠
cons reflect context-specific human moral judgments. Consequently, the eMFD facilitates 
the calculation of morality scores for each tweet by comparing tweet content with lexicons 
in the ten-dimensional moral foundations dictionary.
2.3‚Äā Frame analysis
To analyze how Congress members frame climate change on Twitter, we employed a hybrid 
computer-assisted approach that integrates corpus linguistics techniques with deductive 
manual classification (Nicholls and Culpepper 2020). This approach began with identifying 
potential frames and associated framing devices through linguistic analysis (Koteyko et al. 
2010). We then proceeded to a deductive stage where elements were coded based on theory-
driven frameworks, and subsequently classified the original corpus using frame lexicons 
(Nicholls and Culpepper 2020). To identify the lexical prominence within the broad corpus, 
we initially extracted a list of keywords (Sinclair 1991; Baker et al. 2013) by comparing 
their frequency against the AmE06 reference corpus (Potts and Baker 2012), thereby isolat¬≠
ing distinctive lexical elements of Congress members‚Äô climate discourse on Twitter.
Using corpus linguistic techniques, we extracted the top 100 keywords, adhering to 
established norms to offer a representative overview of the corpus (Baker et al. 2013). In 
the subsequent qualitative categorization, we grouped these top 100 keywords into semanti¬≠
cally coherent sets (Van Gorp 2007) and then deductively coded the semantic sets into six 
established frames of climate change discourse. Next, we conducted collocation analysis,

3.1‚Äā Moral foundations expression
We assessed the moral foundations alignment in climate-related tweets by Congress mem¬≠
bers by employing the eMFD. A higher similarity score indicates a greater degree of moral

form a new dataset. This process was repeated 1,000 times to generate multiple new datasets,

4‚Äā Discussion
This study delved into the 111th to 117th U.S. Congress members' Twitter posts on climate 
change, uncovering the nuanced relationship between moral expressions, political ideology, 
and issue framing. Through MFT-based computational analysis, we quantitatively assessed 
the prevalence of individualizing versus binding values among Congress members and the 
emphasis on moral language use (i.e. vices vs. virtues) of Democrats and Republicans. Criti¬≠
cal discourse analysis was further used to examine the sociolinguistic characteristics of 
tweets from Congress members, focusing on the partisan polarization of climate change 
over time, and the relationships between moral foundation profiles, issue frames, and politi¬≠
cal ideology. This multifaceted approach allows us to uncover the dynamic nature of politi¬≠
cal communication about climate change, highlighting not just the content but the frequency 
and context in which these moral expressions were made. Our research investigates the 
bipartisan preferences for climate change framing and moral language, uncovering the intri¬≠

First, our findings reveal a bipartisan tendency towards individualizing moral values,

in framing the climate change, thus having the potential to impact public discourse and to 
influence policy debates and public opinion on climate change.
Second, both parties appeared to sustain their longstanding positions on climate change, 
as partisan framing strategies generally appeared to align with the agendas of the respective 
parties. Democrats continued to advocate for timely climate change action, as they tended 
to employ the ‚ÄúRunaway Science‚ÄĚ frame to emphasize the exigency of climate change and 
the need for immediate action. Meanwhile, Republicans consistently cast doubts on the real 
impacts of climate change and highlighted the economic costs of pursuing environmental 
regulations, while opting for the ‚ÄúMiddle Way‚ÄĚ frame to advocate more balanced or recon¬≠
ciliatory solutions.
Third, we identified clear partisan divides in framing climate change through a series 
of chi-square tests. This polarization has intensified over time, from the 111th to the 117th 
congresses. Nonetheless, we have also noted areas where common ground might be found. 
Although Republicans‚Äô participation in climate change discourse has traditionally lagged, 
this may be changing. Specifically, Republicans have also supported pro-environmental 
development through the ‚ÄúMiddle Way‚ÄĚ approach that advocates clean energy technologies 
like nuclear, storage, geothermal and carbon capture, as well as legislations for emission 
mitigation and clean energy.
Fourth, our study shows that the use of moral language (vice vs. virtue) was associated 
with climate change frames; by contrast, the effects of partisanship and presidential incum¬≠
bency on this association were relatively minor. For example, a ‚ÄúMiddle Way‚ÄĚ framing may 
highlight virtue values to encourage participation in collective action, and conversely, vice 
words may be used in the ‚ÄúRunaway Science‚ÄĚ frame to deter public engagement (Moser 
2007; Wlodarczyk et al. 2017; Markowitz and Shariff 2012). This insight contributes to 
a deeper understanding of the complexities in the communication strategies employed by 
Democrats and Republicans, particularly regarding the use of moral language within spe¬≠
cific frames.
Fifth, our findings offer a counterpoint to the traditional view that moral values align 
strictly with partisan ideologies by suggesting that moral considerations in climate discus¬≠
sions are more topic-specific and dynamic than previously thought. As mentioned above, 
our analysis indicates that specific issue frames are more strongly associated with the use 
of moral language than party affiliation in the climate change debate, with the influence 
of party affiliation and presidential incumbency being comparatively minor. This suggests 
the potential for ‚Äúideological symmetry in moral motives,‚ÄĚ where both parties may frame

Our research suggests that engaging in climate discussions through shared moral values 
could foster bipartisan dialogue and support for pro-environment policies. The flexibility in 
moral foundations observed among Democrats and Republicans indicates that moral values, 
far from being static ideological markers, are dynamically employed by politicians to navi¬≠

could serve as a potent tool in bridging ideological divides, particularly in the context of the

might otherwise find common ground. Morality can function as a strong motivating factor in 
potentially moving beyond the partisan divide to reach a level of general consensus.
In addition, our findings indicate that when it comes to climate issues, these elites‚Äô use 
of moral language can change over time with the rise and fall in parties‚Äô political power. As 
such, future scholarly endeavors should be wary of the limitations of a static conception of 
moral claims around climate issues. As Democrats and Republicans may use different moral 
language in climate discourse in response to different circumstances, a more dynamic view 
of moral language use will likely be more instructive.
5‚Äā Conclusion
This study represents a preliminary effort to apply a combination of computational text 
analysis and critical discourse analysis to examine moral foundations in climate change dis¬≠
cussions. Our findings reveal that, despite the partisan divide surrounding climate change, 
both Democrats and Republicans converge in moral values and the moral language use 
related to climate issues. Interestingly, Republicans are moving away from outright denial 
of climate change towards a more reconcilable ‚ÄúMiddle Way‚ÄĚ to this contentious issue. 
This shift challenges the prevailing conception that political opinions on climate change in 
the U.S. are irreconcilably polarized, highlighting several practical approaches for bridging 
the partisan divide. First, pro-environmental advocates can underscore the new economic 
opportunities (e.g., in clean energy industries) that climate actions may generate to engage 
political conservatives. Second, given the shared pattern of moral communication concern¬≠
ing the climate issues, promoting bipartisan dialogue on climate actions is crucial to uniting 
leaders and citizens across the partisan divide. The findings from this study offer a glimmer 
of hope for collective action against climate change in the U.S.
It is important to recognize several limitations of the present study. First, our analysis is 
primarily based on Twitter data due to limitations in data availability. Future studies may 
incorporate social media data from other platforms for a more comprehensive examination 
of Congress members‚Äô persuasion strategies on climate change. Comparative studies that
