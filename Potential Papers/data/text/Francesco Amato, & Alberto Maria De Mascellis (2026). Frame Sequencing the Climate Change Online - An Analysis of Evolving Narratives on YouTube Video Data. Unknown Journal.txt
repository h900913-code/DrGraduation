Frame Sequencing the Climate Change 
Online: An Analysis of Evolving Narratives 
on YouTube Video Data 
 
 
Abstract 
 
Over the past decades, YouTube has become a central arena for public 
narratives where global risks, such as climate change, are increasingly 
framed for online audiences. We present empirical research conducted 
through a novel Framing Analysis approach, which we define “Frame 
Sequencing”, aimed at analysing large volumes of unstructured data, 
particularly valuable for qualitative and quantitative inquiries on audiovisual 
content online. Methodologically, we offer a replicable workflow aimed at a 
robust Framing Analysis on user-generated video data, showing how context 
evolution shapes the prominence and articulation of online communication 
on the platform.  
Finally, the proposed framework has been proven on YouTube platform 
following the discussed issue of climate change, where it insightfully 
highlighted the frame sequences on which a qualitative deepening has been 
carried out, leading to the acknowledgment of three evolving narratives 
portrayed by influential platform actors: Data room narratives; Science and 
call to action; and climate change political debate. 
 
 
Keywords: Framing Analysis (1); Methodology (2); Platform Narratives (3); 
Video data (4); Climate Change (5) 
 
 
1 Introduction 
 
YouTube has become a central arena for public narratives about climate 
change, where content creation can be used as a form of audiovisual Framing 
to shape how global risks and debates are explained and thematised to the 
general audience online (Gamson and Modigliani 1989; Carvalho 2010; 


Schäfer and Schlichting 2014; Dudo and Atkinson 2017). On YouTube, 
issues such as climate change are made visible, interpreted and negotiated 
through audiovisual narratives that combine scientific information, aesthetic 
choices and engagement-oriented formats (Dudo and Atkinson 2017; León 
et al. 2018). Nevertheless, most studies of climate change communication on 
social media still privilege posts and comments for analysis or focus on 
narrower contexts (Schäfer and Schlichting 2014; Pearce et al. 2019), leaving 
unexplored how Framing practices evolve over time, especially on platforms 
that require their content creators to develop specific strategies for content 
production (León et al. 2018; Reynolds and Hallinan 2024; Scharlach et al. 
2024). On YouTube, climate change-related content is not simply presented 
as static information: instead, it emerges from ongoing negotiations 
regarding what constitutes relevant evidence, credible expertise, and 
legitimate definitions of the problem. Literature recognises this process as 
Framing (Entman 1993; Nisbet 2009; Carvalho 2010). Since video data has 
proven a valuable resource in qualitative research (Wang and Lien 2013), we 
conducted a longitudinal Framing Analysis of the platform, as it effectively 
captures a diverse range of User Generated Content (UGC) created not only 
by end-users but also by media organisations, independent experts, and 
media activists. 
Previous research on Framing Analysis has shown how public issues are 
organised through configurations that select certain aspects of reality, 
connect them in causal links chains and make particular solutions appear 
more or less appropriate based on context (Goffman 1974; Entman 2003; Pan 
and Kosicki 1993; Scheufele 1999), then used to influence other journalists 
and even governments into specific agendas (Riker 1986).  
In the field of climate change communication, these configurations 
influence how risks are perceived, how responsibility is attributed and how 
mitigation or adaptation pathways are imagined (Carvalho 2010; Schäfer and 
Schlichting 2014; Guenther et al. 2023). On YouTube, such frames are 
articulated over time through verbal argumentation as well as via visual 
repertoires, editing choices, tone of voice and interaction with audiences in 
comment sections, making the platform a key site for observing how climate 
change is articulately constructed as a public problem in the digital 
mediascape (Burgess and Green 2018; León et al. 2018). 
Considering that “Framing provides the context that activates prior 
knowledge” (Cappella and Jamieson 1997) and newsmakers organise frames 
into the so-called “trains of thoughts”, where context emerges over time 
(Price et al. 1997), we structured a longitudinal Framing Analysis to achieve

the most nuanced representation of frames over time while providing a solid 
base for further interpretations.  
This temporal frame also presents substantive contextual relevance 
because it coincides with a period marked by COVID-19 and accelerated 
shifts in public discourse, during which climate change was publicly 
discussed and meaningfully re-articulated. During this phase, climate change 
communication circulated within a broader agenda shaped by overlapping 
crises, constituting a salient issue environment (Klenert et al. 2020; Zang et 
al. 2021). Additionally, the U.S. presidential election in 2017 and 2024 
formed part of the contemporaneous context within which climate debates 
circulated in international media. Rather than estimating event-level effects, 
we interpret observed longitudinal shifts in YouTube climate narratives 
against this documented socio-political backdrop (De Pryck and Gemenne 
2017; Sun and Ye 2025). 
Therefore, we selected English-language videos from October 2020 to 
October 2025 as the temporal range for corpus construction. A five-year 
interval is widely recognised as a suitable timeframe for longitudinal 
analyses in media and communication research, as it allows the identification 
of both structural patterns and short-term discursive fluctuations, while 
remaining sufficiently contained to ensure analytical coherence (Saldaña 
2003; Lüders et al. 2010; Rogers 2013).  
Using video captions as a textual proxy for audiovisual content (Li et al. 
2025), this research combines distributional semantics (Lenci and Sahlgren 
2023) and hierarchical clustering with in-depth qualitative video analysis on 
an empirically informed subsample. Positioned within longitudinal 
scholarship on frame dynamics and temporal variation, our approach 
complements established efforts to track how frames change over time by 
moving from predefined frame categories to inductively detected trajectories 
grounded in platform data (de Vreese 2005; Matthes 2009). This novel 
approach, which we refer to as “Frame Sequencing” (defined in section 
§3.1), integrates distributional semantics with close qualitative examination, 
enabling the identification of temporal regularities that neither a purely 
computational analysis nor a purely qualitative reading would reveal on its 
own. In doing so, it also speaks to research on issue attention and public 
problem competition by treating shifts in narrative organisation as occurring 
within an evolving issue environment rather than as isolated textual 
fluctuations (Hilgartner & Bosk 1988). The sequential patterns detected 
through this combined procedure emerge only when lexical dynamics are 
examined across time and then interpreted through detailed inspection of the 


underlying audiovisual content. The results show how the interplay between 
socio-political dynamics and platform logics contributes to reshaping the 
boundaries between descriptive, mobilising and agonistic forms of climate 
change communication (Gillespie 2010; Scharlach et al. 2024). These 
dynamics are thus analysed as platform-shaped processes, as YouTube’s 
infrastructural conditions influence what becomes legible, comparable, and 
visible over time (Helmond 2015; Plantin et al. 2018). 
The article is structured as follows. The first section presents Framing 
Analysis and situates YouTube climate change narratives within the broader 
socio-political and media context of the 2020-2025 period; the second one 
presents the methodological design; the third section reports the main 
findings, introducing the concept of “Frame Sequencing” and reconstructing 
the three that were found and their evolution over time. The discussion 
examines the results, highlighting the interrelationship between climate 
change communication and contemporary socio-political debates, while the 
conclusion outlines the main implications for future studies on climate 
change communication on YouTube. 
 
 
2 Framing Analysis on YouTube, an ever-changing context 
 
In the now classic text, “Frame Analysis: An Essay on the Organization of 
Experience” (1974), Erving Goffman reflects on the question: «under what 
circumstances do we believe things to be real?», borrowing the term “frame” 
from Bateson (1972), who uses it to describe the “serious” situations that 
arise during fantasy games, and which the author defines as “parentheses” 
within which something is real for those who participate. Aiming at a more 
operative definition, Entman (1993), following Fiske and Taylor (1991), 
clarifies that: «frames highlight certain bits of information about a 
communicated object, increasing its salience». The word “salience” means 
making a piece of information more recognisable, significant, and 
memorable to the audience. An increase in salience improves the likelihood 
that receivers will perceive the information, understand and process its 
meaning and store it in memory (Entman 1991). The temporal dimension is 
also present in the work of Reese et al. (2001), who look at frames as 
organising principles that are socially shared and enduring over time, able to 
work symbolically and significantly structure the social world.

On the other hand, YouTube has become one of the most vibrant 
environments for bringing social and ethical issues to global attention, not 
only as a participatory space but as a socio-technical system where ranking 
metrics, content moderation, and creator-economy incentives condition what 
circulates and how it is formatted (Gillespie 2010; van Dijck et al. 2018; 
Bucher 2018; Cunningham and Craig, 2019). Issues related to healthcare 
(Johnson et al. 2019), fake news and hate speech (Lee et al. 2021; Sauntson 
2025) geopolitical tensions and propaganda (Dekker and Nguyen 2020) 
among others, have been discussed and negotiated on the platform over the 
years (Lewis and Christin 2022). 
There is also a vast amount of research techniques for carrying out a 
Framing Analysis on legacy media, many of which are summarised in a 
literature review by Ali and Hassan (2022), and others of more recent 
conception (Hamborg 2023), but carrying out an analysis on YouTube means 
both considering its main characteristics and adapting the analysis techniques 
to an ever-changing environment. 
Among the main differences, of course, is the audiovisual dimension, 
with meanings articulated by images and sounds that would be impossible to 
present in legacy media (at least in the same quantity), a phenomenon 
reported as multimodality which «involves complex uses of spoken 
language, written language, visualisations in diagrammatic and pictorial 
form (increasingly often dynamic), as well as forms of metacommunication 
(spoken pointers, manual gestures, dynamic presentation resources such as 
zooms, slide transitions, gradual text reveals) that explicitly synchronises the 
diverse combinations employed» (Bateman et al. 2021). Although analysing 
captions means working on texts, these derive from transcriptions of 
dialogues that transform originally multimodal communicative practices into 
written artefacts, which differ from traditional textual production (Ochs 
1979; Bucholtz 2000; Flewitt et al. 2009; Mondada 2018; Dancygier 2023) 
and require dedicated analysis. Among the similarities, however, is one of 


the most important factors we mentioned earlier: the temporal axis. On 
YouTube, temporality is also infrastructural, because visibility is 
continuously 
recalibrated 
through 
ranking, 
recency 
signals, 
and 
recommendation, which can amplify or dampen narrative formats over time 
(Bucher 2018; Covington et al. 2016). According to the literature reported so 
far, a framing attempt must be continuous over time, making the user fill the 
empty “slots” with information on a given subject, thus updating their 
knowledge (van Dijk, Kintsch 1983). As in Scheufele (2004) «For example, 
someone who reads an article about the situation in the Middle East has 
already formed an idea of the situation. He will change it, however, refining 
and redrawing it on the basis of the information contained in the article». 
When addressing phenomena such as climate change, this calls for a 
longitudinal framing analysis that tracks how narratives evolve, especially 
during significant transformations. This approach should also produce 
results that can be examined through in-depth qualitative methods. The 
methodological choices that support this research design are outlined in the 
following section. 
 
 
3 Research design 
 
The main research questions that guided this study are: how global climate 
change communication on YouTube has evolved between 2020 and 2025, 
and whether, over this time span, some framing strategies have been 
continuously reproduced or reformulated. To address these research 
questions, we conducted an empirical study using a novel approach to 
analyse large volumes of unstructured YouTube data on climate change 
communication, applying it to a five-year corpus of sufficiently long videos 
to advance framing analysis techniques for audiovisual content online. For 
each video, we extracted the captions and analysed them using a 
computational method, followed by a subsequent qualitative deepening.

The corpus was constructed through systematic daily queries between   
October 1st, 2020, and October 1st, 2025, to the YouTube API with the 
keyword “climate change”. This strategy approximates a search-surfaced 
issue space rather than an exhaustive archive of all climate-related uploads, 
as results may be shaped by query design, indexing practices, and 
undocumented changes in search ranking and relevance criteria over time. 
The initial extraction yielded more than 50,000 videos.  From this substantial 
dataset, we retained only videos that met three selection criteria: English-


language captions1, at least one user comment, and an explicit indication of 
English as the only admitted language. More than a mere “subset”, the corpus 
retrieved was intended as a platform-conditioned population produced by 
affordances and unequal conditions of legibility and participation: caption 
availability and “commentability” are unevenly distributed across channels, 
therefore shaping which climate narratives become computationally 
processable and socially responsive within the platform’s public space 
(Helmond 2015; van Dijck et al. 2018; Gillespie 2018). Applying these filters 
resulted in a dataset of 1,729 videos. To reflect platform logics, we 
distinguished short videos (≤180s) from long videos (>180s) and focused the 
analysis on the long-form videos (N=1,171).  
Short videos were treated as a separate category and excluded from the 
analysis. This decision reflects a precise methodological consideration: 
because framing analysis requires sufficient discursive material to identify a 
coherent configuration of framing functions, highly compressed content 
often under-specify these elements and can reduce the validity and stability 
of frame inferences (Entman 1993; Matthes and Kohring 2008; Krippendorff 
2018). In the YouTube context, short-form videos follow platform-specific 
logics, rapid consumption, compressed argumentation, and distinct 
aesthetics that differ substantially from long-form videos (Chen et al. 2024). 
Accordingly, we treat the exclusion of short-form content not only as a scope 
delimitation but as an analytic distinction between format regimes. Long-
form videos enable extended evidentiary narration and cumulative framing 
work (De Lara et al. 2017), whereas short-form videos are typically 
optimized for high-velocity attention and different conventions of visibility, 
circulation, and engagement (Chen et al. 2024; Zulli and Zulli 2020). 
Integrating both formats would therefore conflate distinct platform 
grammars rather than increase corpus coverage under comparable analytical 
conditions. 
No further exclusions were applied beyond the criteria described above, 
and the final dataset therefore reflects the sample of climate-change-related 
videos meeting these platform-derived conditions. Captions were gathered 
through a third-party provider that utilises the YouTube API to extract them

through iterative viewing, note-taking, and the compilation of analytic 
memos (Kalogeratos and Likas 2011). Selecting a small set of centroid-
nearest cases per cluster was intended to balance coverage of cluster 
variation with the practical constraints of in-depth audiovisual interpretation 
(Tracy 2010). The interpretive process followed a systematic, iterative 
approach in which we compared our observations across videos and clusters. 
Although the study did not employ a formal multi-coder protocol, the 
analytical categories were refined through internal cross-checking and 
discussion among the authors to enhance interpretive consistency, and we 
documented this process through a shared analytical template and an audit 
trail to improve transparency and traceability of interpretive decisions. The 
combination of descriptive salience profiles and close examination of the 
prototype videos supported a more robust interpretation of the clusters and 
the detection of recurrent Framing Sequences. The sole computational 
analysis would have yielded clusters with limited capacity to account for the 
underlying phenomena. At the same time, the sole qualitative approach could 
not capture the quantity of videos we selected. The triangulated procedure 
offered analytical depth that neither approach could achieve on its own, 
enabling a deeper detection of recurring framing sequences in climate change 
narratives on YouTube, which is the focus of the subsequent sections of this 
article. 
 
4 Findings 
 
4.1 Introducing the frame sequencing 
 
Here we introduce the concept of “Frame sequencing”, a novel Framing 
Analysis approach which involves identifying coherent narrative segments 
and their patterned ordering as they emerge and evolve during the selected 


 
11 
timespan. Therefore, in this study we use the term Framing Sequences to 
denote dynamic configurations that trace the temporal unfolding of framing 
practices across the five-year corpus. Specifically, while a “frame” refers to 
a relatively stable pattern to suggest a problem definition, causal 
interpretation, and normative orientation (Entman 1993; Scheufele 1999), a 
Framing Sequence captures how such patterns recur, mutate, or realign over 
time within the platform’s evolving discursive environment. The analytical 
need for a “sequence” construct arises when longitudinal change is not 
adequately described as variation in frame prevalence alone, but as the re-
composition of framing contents into recurrent temporal arrangements, such 
as stable problem presentations, recurring evidentiary segments, shifting 
attributions of responsibility, and the periodic reappearance of action-
oriented closures. In this sense, “sequence” is a higher-order construct with 
three minimal properties: recurrence, the same functional arrangement 
reappears across snapshots; mutability, the arrangement persists while 
internal elements shift in emphasis or lexicon; and realignment, the 
arrangement changes in response to contextual and platform conditions, 
producing detectable re-orderings of framing functions. 
The focus is not only on which frames are present, but on how framing is 
assembled and re-assembled over time through relatively recognisable 
narrative configurations. It is also complementary to research on issue-
attention cycles (Downs 2016), because it treats attention shifts as conditions 
under which particular framing arrangements become more likely to 
stabilise, fragment, or polarise, rather than as explanations of content change 
per se (Hilgartner and Bosk 1988; Matthes,2009). 
Operationally, this approach differs from existing longitudinal framing 
studies in news media (de Vreese 2005; Matthes 2009; Cobo et al. 2025), 
which typically track predefined frame categories over time. Here, sequences 
emerge inductively from a combination of distributional patterns and 
qualitative interpretation, allowing the analysis to capture platform-specific 
dynamics, such as creator practices, audiovisual conventions and 
algorithmically shaped visibility, that play a constitutive role in how climate 
narratives evolve on YouTube. To qualify a pattern as a Framing Sequence 
in this study, we required converging evidence across three layers: repeated 
cluster-level lexical signatures across multiple snapshots (e.g., recurring 
salient term profiles rather than one-off topical spikes); consistent 
multimodal-narrative organisation in the prototype videos (e.g., comparable 
ordering of framing functions and recurrent presentational conventions); and 
an interpretable mechanism linking the observed persistence or shift to 


platform and socio-political conditions (e.g., changes in creator strategies, 
genre conventions, or visibility dynamics).  
Our Frame Sequencing proposes a novel way to theorise longitudinal 
framing on platformised media as the evolution of recurrent narrative 
arrangements are shaped by both issue environments and platform logics, 
rather than as a mere rise-and-fall of issues. 
 
4.2 Results 
 
We enriched the computational analysis of the most frequent words in each 
cluster with a qualitative interpretation based on an in-depth inspection of 
the 54 videos that emerged. We tracked the longitudinal development of the 
terms associated with each cluster, emphasising those that frequently co-
occurred over time or shared conceptual linkages, which contributed to 
defining each Framing Sequence. We treated terms from both higher-order 
and lower-order categories, as well as lexical variations and changes in 
discursive roles, as parts of the same underlying narrative sequence. 
For visualisation purposes, we limited the graphical representation to the 
twenty most frequent terms per cluster, out of the over a hundred proposed. 
The result presented in Figure 2 is based on this analytical process, using the 
twenty most frequent terms as guidelines. The colour distinctions that 
differentiate the framing sequences were conceptually defined only after the 
in-depth inspection of the selected videos. 
Based on the aforementioned research approach, the analysis of the five-
year snapshots reveals three recurring Framing sequences that shape how 
climate change is narrated and addressed on the platform. These sequences 
are: (a) “Data Room Narratives”, (b) “Science and Call to Action: Civic 
Mobilisation”, and (c) “Climate Change Debate: Crisis, Adaptation, and 
Answers”. These are not static categories, but dynamic narrative structures: 
their prominence, internal structure, and degree of overlapping terms change 
over time. This evolution reflects broader shifts in public attention, political 
context, and platform dynamics. Each sequence arises from specific lexical 
trends identified through clustering and from the narrative, argumentative, 
and performative characteristics observed in the qualitative subsample.  
Taken together, these three framing sequences provide a coherent 
analytical map for understanding how climate change is discursively 
organised on YouTube over time for the Anglophone public. The framing 
sequences allowed the qualitative reconstruction of the dominant narrative 
trajectories and the subtle shifts in emphasis, alignment, and conflict that

characterise the platform’s evolving discourse. This framework serves as the 
basis for the detailed examination presented in the following sections, where 
each framing sequence is analysed in depth.

or discursive conflict. 
Overall, our figure, while representing an overview of the process, makes 
visible the intersection between lexicon, temporality, and discursive 
configurations. The result is a diachronic framework that allows us to read 
the transformation in the ways YouTube frames climate change through 
three distinct but intertwined narratives. 
 
 
4.3 Data Room Narratives 
 
The first sequence is characterised by a data-driven communication style that 
focuses on providing information mostly drawn from climate change reports, 
in a documentary style. Videos in this category function as “data rooms”3: 
they reconstruct climate change through the visual grammar of expert 
briefings, combining scientific reports, geophysical modelling, satellite 
imagery and 3D graphic representations of the Earth system. This register 
foregrounds not only the authority of data but also its performative staging, 
in which the video makers adopt visual conventions reminiscent of 
documentary science communication, repeatedly anchoring the narrative in 
technical evidence. As a result, climate change is framed primarily as a

quantifiable, measurable and model-based phenomenon, whose risks can be 
understood by mastering the underlying data.  
Within this frame, content creators tend to position themselves as 
intermediaries of scientific expertise, employing a deductive architecture that 
moves from the planetary scale to case-specific implications. The narrative 
rhythm is linear and didactic, emphasising clarity, step-by-step explanation 
and an impersonal tone. Climate change is thus framed less as a contested 
political issue and more as a technical problem to be correctly represented. 
 
 
4.4 Science and Call to Action: Civic Mobilisation 
 
The second framing sequence is centred on mobilisation. In these videos, 
scientific knowledge is reinterpreted through a pragmatic, future-oriented 
lens, with the purpose not simply to describe the climate crisis but to translate 
understanding into action. The framing logic is moving from evidence to 
implication and from knowledge to commitment. This communication style 
became evident in the 2021-2022 phase, when the pandemic context 
amplified the connection between scientific communication and civic 
mobilisation, and much of the climate change conversation circulated 
through online conferences, remote seminars and digital public events. 
During that period, the online environment, urging individuals to take 
concrete measures to protect their health, also framed how the climate 
change narrative was communicated, spurring mobilisation, partly due to 
signs of environmental improvement stemming from near-zero use of fossil 
fuels. This call-to-action framing, although it has seen some shifts in the 
subsequent years, has remained stable in its communicative style.  
In this register, content creators integrated expert input with explicit 
invitations to act, ranging from “how-to” guides on sustainable practices to 
broader appeals for collective responsibility. The narrative architecture tends 
to follow a problem–solution pattern, in which a scientific exposition is 
immediately followed by a set of actionable pathways, often illustrated 
through community initiatives, policy proposals, or technological 
innovations. Audiovisually, these videos assume a more relational tone by 
directly addressing, with emotionally coded pacing that establishes a sense 
of shared purpose. The videos may also incorporate fragments of online 
events (such as conferences, panel discussions, and webinars) that reflect a 
moment when the boundaries between scientific dissemination, advocacy, 
and civic education were unusually permeable. 


In the last snapshots, this framing sequence becomes more overtly 
political: initially, the videos tend to emphasise individual responsibility and 
possible behavioural adjustments, but later ones begin to situate climate 
action within debates about justice, governance, and inequality. Therefore, 
the framing evolves from the appeals to reduce emissions or adopt 
sustainable habits to the reflections on institutional accountability and the 
uneven distribution of climate risks. This shift mirrors a broader 
transformation in public discourse. As the socio-political implications of 
climate change become more salient and the pandemic’s communicative 
environment recedes, mobilisation is no longer framed merely as a personal 
choice but as a collective task requiring coordinated social and political 
intervention. In this sense, the framing sequence captures the transition from 
an early phase in which civic appeals emerge organically within digital 
scientific spaces to a later phase where calls to action articulate a more 
explicit theory of conflict and institutional change. 
 
 
4.5 Climate Change Debate: Crisis, Adaptation, and Answers 
 
The third framing sequence is defined by explicit instances of confrontation 
and by overtly argumentative, polemical, or politically charged content. 
These videos do not merely present information but actively enter the arena 
of climate change debate, addressing and replying to denialist claims 
spreading, and tackling narratives that minimise the severity of the crisis. 
Rather than focusing on technical explanation or mobilisation, they articulate 
climate change as a discursive battleground structured by conflict, 
disagreement and competition for epistemic authority. 
This register becomes increasingly polarised as the snapshots move to the 
later years, reflecting the progressive politicisation of climate change 
discourse, particularly in the United States, from the period marked by the 
2022 midterm elections to the 2024 presidential campaign. The videos’ 
communication style is structured after talk shows, online political 
commentary, or podcast-style debates, with a multi-voice format featuring 
hosts, commentators, and recurring ideological antagonists. In this setting, 
climate change is addressed less as a scientific object and more as a proxy 
for broader political identities, cultural alignments and partisan conflicts. As 
these formats invade the YouTube climate space, the resulting discourse 
becomes more agonistic, with content creators positioning themselves as 


 
17 
participants in a contested public sphere rather than neutral disseminators of 
information. 
Rhetorically, videos in this sequence rely on a dialectical approach, in 
which claims are introduced, challenged, reframed and contrasted. Content 
creators often alternate between confutation and demonstration, combining 
selective data with narrative tactics designed to expose inconsistencies, 
reveal logical fallacies or highlight the political motivations underlying 
sceptical positions. This style oscillates between patient explanation and 
openly confrontational commentary, depending on the content creator’s 
persona and intended audience. Crucially, denialist narratives are not simply 
dismissed but used instead as discursive catalysts through which creators 
reaffirm scientific legitimacy or articulate concerns about institutional 
responsibility, political obstruction, or the consequences of policy inaction. 
The expansion of this framing sequence over the snapshots is not merely 
a by-product of platform dynamics but reflects a broader historical and 
political shift. In this framing sequence, the discursive emphasis shifts from 
the risks documented by scientific evidence to those generated by political 
inaction, partisan obstruction, and strategic misinformation. Likewise, here 
the question of responsibility is reframed, as it no longer concerns only 
individual behaviours or national emissions trajectories, but also the actors 
who shape public understanding through conflictual narratives. The race to 
lead the climate change debate becomes a competition of imaginaries about 
the future, facing each other through narratives of catastrophe, denialism, 
resilience, and techno-solutionism. 
 
5 Discussion  
 
The evolution of the three framing sequences across the snapshots of the five-
year corpus reflects a complex interplay between content creators’ 
communication style and crucial socio-political and historical developments. 
This temporal span appears to coincide with a period marked by overlapping 
crises that repeatedly reshaped public agendas.  
In the first framing sequence, the use of a scientific register reflects a 
communication style that resonates with audiences familiarised with 
documentary-like formats. In this sense, the risk is framed primarily as a 
physical, measurable phenomenon. At the same time, responsibility is 
associated with institutional and macro-level actors, and mitigation is seen 
as a technical pathway aligned with scientific feasibility. 


The second framing sequence foregrounds the socio-political 
environment, which grew unstable, leading to the urge for mobilisation-
oriented narratives. Online conferences, remote activism, and the revamping 
of public debates created favourable conditions for content that translated 
scientific insights into behavioural cues, ethical appeals, and visions of 
collective responsibility. The framing of risk became more situated and 
socially grounded, connecting climatic impacts to inequalities, community 
vulnerability and intergenerational concerns. Mitigation, in turn, was 
reframed as a domain of civic engagement and moral agency rather than a 
purely technical trajectory.  
The third framing sequence emphasises political contrasts that shifted the 
salience and emotional charge of climate change discourse, highlighting 
similarities between the analysed climate change communication and that 
observed in previous years (Marshall 2015). The 2024 U.S.A. presidential 
election added another layer of volatility in the observed debate, amplifying 
polarisation around environmental policy and reinforcing the visibility of 
antagonistic narratives. Responsibility shifted towards communicative 
behaviour and political obstruction, while mitigation became an arena of 
ideological dispute.  
Taken together, the three framing sequences show the dynamic 
representation of climate change across YouTube, where different 
communication styles have been employed to address the convergence of 
scientific development, activism and geopolitical events.  
This diachronic perspective underscores how climate change 
communication on YouTube continually adapts to the socio-political 
environment in which it is embedded, making the platform a key site for 
observing the reconfiguration of contemporary climate narratives. 
 
6 Conclusion 
 
The analysis results show how on YouTube thrives an evolving and 
heterogeneous 
discursive 
ecosystem 
in 
which 
climate 
change 
communication is articulated through shifting combinations of scientific 
explanation, civic mobilisation and public contestation. 
The proposed methodological approach highlights the value of analysing 
climate narratives on YouTube through a sequential and temporally sensitive 
research design. The contribution of this approach is to reveal how frames 
evolve over time, making visible patterns of divergence and realignment that 
would remain hidden in cross-sectional analyses of climate communication. 


 
19 
By tracing the trajectory of these framing sequences across the snapshots, 
the study offers an empirically grounded account of how platform dynamics 
and socio-political events shape the evolution of climate-related discourses. 
The combination of hierarchical clustering on vectorised video captions and 
in-depth qualitative video analysis enables the reconstruction of narrative 
configurations that would be difficult to identify through a single analytic 
lens. Thus, given the volume of videos uploaded daily on these platforms, 
this approach shows a replicable framework for future studies seeking to 
examine the complexity of contemporary world communication within 
platformised media ecosystems (Mattes and Kohring 2008; Salganik 2019). 
The multimodal nature of content on platform-based systems such as 
YouTube poses specific analytical challenges. Any attempt to study these 
videos must consider how spoken language, written captions, dynamic 
visualisations, gestures and meta-communicative cues work together 
(Flewitt et al. 2009; Bateman et al. 2021; Mondada 2018). This 
multimodality expands the expressive repertoire through which climate 
change can be framed, enabling narrative density and affective resonance 
that go beyond purely textual formats (Ochs 1979; Bucholtz 2000). At the 
same time, relying on captions for computational analysis introduces a 
structural reduction: captions transcribe spoken interactions but inevitably 
turn complex multimodal practices into linear textual artefacts (Ochs 1979; 
Bucholtz 2000; Flewitt et al. 2009; Mondada 2018). Recognising this tension 
is essential for interpreting our findings, as it highlights both the potential 
and the limits of studying climate change communication on a platform 
where meaning is produced through the interplay of multiple modalities and 
stakeholders. 
For these reasons, our results show how climate change is framed within 
a specific sample of YouTube videos over time, under conditions of 
discursive, political and technological change. By tracing the emergence and 
transformation of the three framing sequences, the study sheds light on the 
processes through which climate change narratives are produced, circulated 
and disputed on this platform.  
The three framing sequences identified in the study capture the main 
trajectories through which a diverse array of stakeholders renders the climate 
issue visible, intelligible and actionable for diverse audiences. Their 
evolution over time reflects the interaction between changes in the socio-
political environment and the adaptive strategies of content producers. Our 
interpretation aligns with prior research on climate change framing in online 
video (León et al. 2018), which shows that framings are not randomly 


distributed but patterned by institutional roles, professional routines, and 
broader political–economic logics that shape which narrative elements are 
foregrounded and which forms of mobilisation are encouraged. In this light, 
the presence of scientific–technical framing to mobilisation-oriented and 
polarised political framing does not merely reflect shifts in stylistic 
preferences: it mirrors a progressive reconfiguration of how different 
stakeholders seek to manage attention, authority and affect in a context of 
overlapping crises. Our three framing sequences can therefore be read as 
specific instantiations of dynamics already identified in the literature, where 
climate narratives move from informational and expert-led registers towards 
more affective, gain/loss-oriented and conflictual representations tightly 
coupled to institutional interests and partisan contestation (O’Neill and 
Nicholson-Cole 2009; Boykoff 2011; Nisbet 2009; Marshall 2015; León et 
al. 2018). 
Future developments of this work could involve the short-video analysis, 
focusing on reels and shorts, a type of video format that is rapidly spreading 
across the principal digital platforms of the today mediascape, or considering 
video comments to study the interactive dynamics that arise between users 
and content creators, eventually employing fine-tuned computational 
approaches to study the visual aspects embedded in the images shown on 
screen.
